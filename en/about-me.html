Skills

SAP

I first came into contact with SAP during my training as an IT specialist for application development.

I was skeptical at first, but now I'm a big fan of SAP! Thanks to my previous knowledge of computer science, I was able to get to grips with ABAP and other technologies very quickly.

Procedural ABAP

My daily work (e.g. reports or interfaces) includes dealing with function modules, form routines or similar.

I myself am a proponent of ABAP OO. Due to legacy code, the procedural ABAP programming model is also part of my repertoire.

ABAP OO

My favorite ABAP programming model is definitely ABAP OO. It offers many advantages over procedural programming if you do it right. It is my first choice for new developments or existing OO developments.

The foundation for object orientation in ABAP was laid by the certificates from the Brandenburg University of Applied Sciences („ABAP for advanced users“) and from the Massachusetts Institute of Technology („Computational Thinking using Python“).

Dynamic Programming

Dynamic programming can seem very scary at first glance. Terms like RTTS, RTTI, RTTC as well as arrows, stars and the good old Elvis Presley operator (?=) are thrown at you. I have to say, I'm now a big fan of it!

Dynamic programming can be used to create very elegant, scalable solutions, when, for example, the basic procedure is the same, but the inputs and outputs can be very different and changeable.

Instead of having to program something 20 - 30 times, just once can be enough! And with good error handling, the program can also be robust and easy to debug.

Dynpro-Programming

I had to deal with Dynpro programming both at work and in the ABAP training course at TH Brandenburg. Terms like PBO, PAI or Screen Painter are part of my vocabulary.

Admittedly, the technology (as well as Web Dynpro) does not come close to front-end web development with e.g. Flexbox or Grid layout.

It still fulfills its purpose for reports or more complex applications.

Reports

Over the years, I have created many reports for all kinds of SAP modules.

Whether procedural or object-oriented. Whether event-oriented programming, dynamic reports or insane customer requests. Everything was included.

I mainly created the reports with the new ALV display (class CL_SALV_TABLE).

ABAP Eclipse

Formerly programmed with the ABAP Workbench. Tried ABAP Eclipse - love at first sight.

ABAP Eclipse is more in line with modern development environments and offers numerous additional functions such as better code refactoring, code editor views, several workspaces and an infinite number of SAP tabs (*Finally no more annoying pop-ups!* üòÑ).

Eclipse also forgives internet crashes. In SAP this is the worst case scenario...

However, I personally find debugging in the ABAP Workbench more convenient.

Clean ABAP

For me, certain development standards such as code refactoring or good readability are part of this.

I know that my code definitely has potential for optimization and I don't always think of everything. Nevertheless, I regularly put a lot of effort into it.

Friend & helpers are the ABAP-Styleguide on Github and the corresponding book „Clean ABAP“ (see section „Books“).

Web Dynpro

I had my first contact with Web Dynpros in the course „ABAP for advanced learners“ of the Brandenburg University of Applied Sciences (see section „Certificates“ for more information).

For example, the Web Dynpro architecture (MVC pattern) and the components (contexts, windows, inbound/outbound plugs, ...) were explained there.

However, I haven't had anything to do with this in the private sector. In my opinion, this is also outdated technology and has been replaced by SAP Fiori.

Form Development

Maintaining forms for all kinds of modules has been a core part of my SAP development career to date.

Mainly the latest SAP forms technology: Adobe Interactive Forms. I have had virtually no contact with the older technologies SAPscript and Smart Forms.

When developing forms, I attach great importance to a very good requirements analysis, documentation and extensive testing.

Depending on customer requirements and the existing code base, a form including printing can range from simple to very elaborate & complex.

SAP-Interface Administration

I have not yet built an SAP interface to other external systems. Only managed and expanded (SAP with the Quipsy quality management software).

It was an interaction of several areas & modules (create purchase order or production order, post goods receipt, check SAP directory AL11, manage server via ASG-Remote Desktop as well as the feedback from Quipsy to SAP).

User & Authorization Administration

Creating, adapting, deactivating & deleting users and authorization checks including authorization roles/objects etc. are also part of my daily job.

Customization of the SAP standard

I had a detailed introduction to customizing the SAP standard in the course „ABAP for advanced users“ of the Brandenburg University of Applied Sciences (see section „Certificates“ for more information).

Search Engine Optimization

A fascinating topic. It's unbelievable how many people want to crack the mysteries of browser algorithms. Just so that a website is visible at the top of the search results.

I dealt with on-page and off-page optimization. Examples of optimizations regarding on-page / on your own website:

  • Loading speed
  • Content such as alt-tags, headings, images, keywords, meta information, ...
  • Internal linking or URL structure

Optimizations regarding off-page / outside your own website were backlinks. Backlinks are links from other external websites to your own website.

There were passive backlinks - links that users voluntarily place on other websites (e.g. because the content of the linked page is very good).

And there were active backlinks - links that you actively build yourself (e.g. guest articles on blogs or partnerships).

Books

The following books make my everyday SAP life easier:

ABAP - The Comprehensive Guide (SAP Press)

Author: Felix Roth (Booklink).

I don't know of any other book that I open more often at work than this one. The Bible.

It provides detailed information on all kinds of topics such as ABAP Dictionary, access to databases, internal tables, reports, Dynpros and much more. more.

Clean ABAP (SAP Press)

Author: Klaus Haeuptle et al. (Booklink).

Clean Code is a term which became known through Robert C. Martin and whose work is known to the majority of software developers.

Code should be written in such a way that it is intuitively understandable. The advantages of clean code are more stable and easily maintainable programs. This in turn means fewer bugs, less maintenance work and less stress. üòâ

I use the book to hone my ABAP programming skills. To be honest, I rarely open the book. That's because I normally have the more condensed associated Github page open.

Getting started with SAP S/4HANA - Business processes, applications, ... (SAP Press)

Authors: Christian Drumm, Bernd Scheuermann, Stefan Weidner (Booklink).

Anyone who programs SAP or ABAP cannot avoid getting to know the functional or economic side.

This book provides the basics for the SAP modules SD, MM, FI, CO, PP and WM.

SAP - The technical introduction (SAP Press)

Authors: Waldemar Fix, Reinhold Plota (Booklink).

A very good book to get to know the SAP technologies and their peculiarities. It offers basic knowledge for customization, development and administration.

ABAP Development in Eclipse (SAP Press)

Author: Daniel Sch√∂n (Booklink).

I was unlucky (or lucky) that the ABAP Workbench was not my first development environment. The first impression: It looks very old-fashioned and lags well behind modern development environments.

My eyes widened when I heard that ABAP could also be developed in Eclipse. For newer SAP technologies, it is even a *must* to develop in Eclipse.

The book made it much easier for me to get started and is now rarely used as a reference book.

SAP Interactive Forms by Adobe (SAP Press)

Author: Timo Ortiz (Booklink).

Illustrates the entire form development process very well and provides helpful tips & tricks. When questions arise, the first thing you do is often turn to the book.
